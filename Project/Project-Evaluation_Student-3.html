Are Internet Use and Civic Engagement Associated

Franc Bračun

17.10.2014

Introduction:

This research examines the relationship between Internet use and civic engagement. Civic engagement is known to catalyze government reform and participatory democracy (Park and Perry , 2008). Informed and empowered citizens are able to facilitate good governance by making all levels of government more efficient, transparent, and accountable. Internet use and online engagement are expected to provide a promising avenue for revitalizing democracy in the digital era. However, relatively little is known about the relationship between Internet use and civic engagement. In this research we will explore the relationship between action-oriented civic engagement and internet use. Action-oriented engagement is going out to do something rather than debating public issues. In particular, this research attempts to answer the following research question: Is there a relationship between whether U.S. citizens of age 18 or older were exposed to the campaign for President on the Internet and their engagement in voluntary community activities?

Data:

This study analyzes nationwide survey data from the American National Election Studies (ANES) collected in election year 2012 (The American National Election Studies (ANES; www.electionstudies.org). The ANES 2012 Time Series Study [dataset]. Stanford University and the University of Michigan [producers]). A survey of voters in the United States has been conducted before and after 2012 presidential election.

Data collection

Data collection was conducted in the two modes (i.e.., face-to-face and Internet) independently, using separate samples. Face-to-face mode is address-based sample with in-person recruitment and interviews. Internet mode is primarily address-based sample and mail recruitment, and Internet interviews. While face-to-face (FTF) respondents were administered the single pre-election interview and single post-election interview traditional to Time Series presidential-election-year studies, for the internet sample the same questions were administered over a total of 4 shorter online interviews, 2 pre-election and 2 post-election. Web-administered cases constituted a representative sample separate from the face-to-face sample and were drawn from panel members of GfK Knowledge Networks.

Units of observation

Cases (i.e., units of observation) are individuals, i.e. voters in US (U.S. citizens age 18 or older). Number of respondents before 2012 presidential election was 5,914 (2,054 face-to-face and 3,860 online), and the number of respondents after 2012 presidential election was 5,510 (1,929 face-to-face and 3,581 online). The data set (http://bit.ly/dasi_anes_data) was extracted from the cumulative data file of ANES 2012.

The type of study

This is retrospective observational study. Namely, data are collected only by monitoring what occurs and does not directly interfere with how the data arise, therefore this is observational study. And since we use past data in our study (we do not collect data) this is retrospective observational study.

Scope of inference - generalizability

The population of interest is U.S. citizens of age 18 or older. The findings from this analysis can be generalized to the population of interest, since (1) in the face-to-face interviews the population of interest was divided into strata (stratifed sampling), and then a multi-stage cluster sampling was employed within each stratum, and (2) in the Web-administered cases simple random sampling was employed (see ANES 2013).

The act of taking the above mentioned sampling methods helps minimize bias, however, bias can crop up in other ways. ANES estimates of voting turnout have been consistently higher than rates reported in aggregate voting returns. Part of this discrepancy is probably due to respondent misreport, which can lead to biased statistical estimation results. ANES has sought to correct this potential bias by independently validating voter turnout. Another common downfall in surveys is if the non-response is high. If only 30% of the people randomly sampled for a survey actually respond, then it is unclear whether the results are representative of the entire population. This non-response bias can skew results. The ANES response rates usually are in the neighborhood of 72%, i.e. non-response rate is less than 30%. However, since the non-response rate is close to 30% caution must be exercised. Therefore we will use weighting to correct skewness in our samples that are meant to represent a population of interest. In ANES data set each weight accounts for the probability of household selection, the probability of respondent selection within the household, nonresponse, and random sampling error. Since we combine face-to-face and internet sample, and we want to achieve generalizibility of the 2012 Time Series data as a representative cross-section, it is necessary to use use weighting. To assign different weights to the different cases in the analysis data we will use “weight_full” weight from ANES data set (i.e., Post-Stratification Weight for the full sample) (see ANES, 2013, pp. 32-37).

Scope of inference - causality

These data can not be used to establish causal links between the variables of interest since this is an observational study. Namely, to establish causal connections one have to randomly assign subjects to treatments. This can be done only in experimental study, not in observational study.

The exploratory data analysis suggests that exposition to the Internet campaign for President (i.e., “netExposure” variable) and engagement in voluntary community activities (i.e., “commuInvolvment” variable) are associated (dependent). We will do this in next section.

Inference:

Hypotheses

We want to determine if the ANES 2012 Time Series Study data set provides strong evidence that the U.S. citizens of age 18 or older who have been exposed to the Internet campaign for President are more likely to engage in voluntary community activities than those who have not been exposed to the Internet campaign for President, versus the other possibility that there is no difference. Our two competing hypotheses are:

H0: There is no difference in community engagement proportions between the U.S. citizens of age 18 or older who have been exposed to the Internet campaign for President and those who have not been exposed to the Internet campaign for President. (pexposed - pnot-exposed = 0)

HA: Community engagement proportion of the U.S. citizens of age 18 or older who have been exposed to the Internet campaign for President is higher than community engagement proportion of those who have not been exposed to the Internet campaign for President. (pexposed - pnot-exposed > 0)

Conditions verification

First, in the code chunk below, we prepare necessary variables for performing conditions check and inference.

n_invExposed <- wTable$t[1,1] # Number of community involved  voters that have been exposed to the Internet campaign 
n_invNotExposed <- wTable$t[2,1] # Number of community involved voters that have not been exposed to the Internet campaign  
nExposed <- wTable$t[1,1] + wTable$t[1,2] # Number of exposed voters
nNotExposed <- wTable$t[2,1] + wTable$t[2,2] # Number of not exposed voters
p_Exposed <- wTable$prop.row[1,1] # Proportion of involved for exposed sample
p_NotExposed <-  wTable$prop.row[2,1] # Proportion of involved for not esposed sample
p_pointEstimate <- p_Exposed- p_NotExposed # Point estimate of the difference between two proportions
p_pooled <- (n_invExposed+n_invNotExposed)/(nExposed+nNotExposed) # Pooled estimate of the sample proportion
data <- c(paste0("                nExposed = ",round(nExposed,2),"  ... Number of exposed voters\n"),
          paste0("            nNotExposed = ",round(nNotExposed,2),"  ... Number of not exposed voters\n"),
          paste0("p_Exposed- p_NotExposed = ",round(p_pointEstimate,2),"  ... Point estimate\n"),

Because each group (i.e., exposed group and not exposed group) is a random sample from less than 10% of U.S. citizens of age 18 or older, ( nexposed = 3028 > 10 and nnot-exposed = 2468 > 10 ), the observations are independent within each group. Since appropriate sampling methods were used in ANES 2012 (see also section Scope of inference - generalizability) there is no reason to expect that the exposed and not exposed group are dependent, and we can conclude that the observations are independent also between the two groups too. Therefore, the independence condition for inference holds.

To check success-failure condition for hypothesis testing for comparing two proportions, we have to use the pooled estimate of the sample proportion. So, the necessary conditions for the sampling distribution of the difference between two proportions being nearly normal are met.

Used method

Since the parameter of interest in our study is the difference between two proportions and the conditions for nearly normal sampling distribution are met, we will use theoretical based method for hypothesis test and confidence interval for two two-level categorical variables.

By this method we would like to make conclusions about the difference in two population proportions. We first identify a reasonable point estimate (p^ exposed - p^ not-exposed) of population parameter (pexposed - pnot-exposed). Next, we verify that the point estimate follows the normal model by checking normality and success-failure conditions (already done above). Finally, we compute the estimate's standard error and apply the normal model for Z test statistic. In the setting of confidence intervals, the sample proportions are used to verify the success-failure condition and also compute standard error. In a hypothesis test, the distribution of the test statistic is always examined as though the null hypothesis (pexposed - pnot-exposed = 0) is true, the standard error formula should reflect this equality in the null hypothesis. However, we don't know the population rate, p, but we can obtain a good estimate of it by pooling the results of both samples. This is called the pooled estimate of the sample proportion, and we use it to compute the standard error when the null hypothesis is that pexposed = pnot-exposed (e.g., pexposed - pnot-exposed = 0 ). We also use the pooled estimate of the sample proportion to verify the success-failure condition for a hypothesis test.

Hypothesis test

The Z test statistic, the pooled standard error and p-value for one-tiled hypothesis test are calculated in the code chunk below.

## Hypothesis test for comparing two proportions
SE_pooled <- sqrt(p_pooled*(1-p_pooled)/nExposed +p_pooled*(1-p_pooled)/nNotExposed)  # Standard error with pooled proportion
z_statistics <- (p_pointEstimate - 0)/SE_pooled # Z statistics for normal distribution
p_value <- pnorm(q=z_statistics, mean=0, sd=SE_pooled, lower.tail=FALSE)# p-value for one-tailed hypothesis test
data <- c(paste0("      SE_pooled = ",round(SE_pooled,3),"  ... the pooled standard error\n"),
          paste0("  z_statistics = ",round(z_statistics,3),"   ... the Z test statistic\n"),
          paste0("       p_value ~ ",round(p_value,2),"      ... p-value\n"))
cat(data)

Since the the p-value is very small (practically zero), there is strong evidence in the data against the null hypothesis (see also the figure above). Namely, if there is no difference in community engagement proportions between the U.S. citizens of age 18 or older who have been exposed to the Internet campaign for President and those who have not been exposed to the Internet campaign for President (i.e., if H0 would be TRUE), there is practically 0% chance of obtaining random samples of 3028 exposed and 2468 not-exposed voters where community engagement proportion of the U.S. citizens of age 18 or older who have been exposed to the Internet campaign for President is higher for 0.12 or more than community engagement proportion of those who have not been exposed to the Internet campaign for President. Therefore, we reject null hypothesis and conclude that the data provide convincing evidence of an alternative hypothesis.

Confidence interval

Since the independence condition for confidence interval and hypothesis test are the same, we have to check only a success-failure condition for confidence interval by using the sample proportions to verify the success-failure condition and compute standard error. As shown (in the excerpt below) calculated values for successes and failures for both groups are much higher than 10. Therefore, success-failure condition for confidence interval calculation also holds. 

exposed_successes <- nExposed*p_Exposed; exposed_failures <- nExposed*(1-p_Exposed) 
non_exposed_successes <- nNotExposed*p_NotExposed; non_exposed_failures <- nNotExposed*(1-p_NotExposed)
data <- c(paste0("    exposed_successes = ",round(exposed_successes),"      "),
          paste0("        exposed_failures = ",round(exposed_failures),"  \n"),

The standard error, critical value (i.e., z*), and 95% confidence interval are calculated in the code chunk below.

## 95% CI for difference between two proportions
SE_ci <- sqrt(p_Exposed*(1-p_Exposed)/nExposed +p_NotExposed*(1-p_NotExposed)/nNotExposed) # Standard error for CI
z_asterix <- qnorm(p=0.975, m=0, sd=1, lower.tail = TRUE) # z* <- 1.96 for 95% CI
CI <- p_pointEstimate + c(-1,1)*z_asterix*SE_ci
data <- c(paste0("        SE_ci = ",round(SE_ci,3),"              ... the standard error for confidence interval\n"),
          paste0("   z_asterix = ",round(z_asterix,3),"               ... critical value (i.e., z* )\n"),
          paste0("      95% CI ~ [ ",round(CI,2)[1]," , ",round(CI,2)[2], " ]    ... 95% confidence interval\n"))
cat(data)

##         SE_ci = 0.013              ... the standard error for confidence interval
##     z_asterix = 1.96               ... critical value (i.e., z* )
##        95% CI ~ [ 0.09 , 0.15 ]    ... 95% confidence interval

We are 95% confident that the proportion of community engaged U.S. citizens of age 18 or older who have been exposed to the Internet campaign for President is between 9% and 15% higher, than the proportion of those who have not been exposed to the Internet campaign for President.

Based on the confidence interval we calculated, we should expect to find the proportion of community engaged U.S. citizens of age 18 or older who have been exposed to the Internet campaign for President to be higher than the proportion of community engaged U.S. citizens of age 18 or older who have not been exposed to the Internet campaign for President. Namely, 95% confidence interval does not include null value. Therefore, the results of hypothesis test and confidence interval calculation agree.

Conclusion:

The results of our study have shed light on the link between the variables of interest in our research question. The analysis revealed strong evidence that the U.S. citizens of age 18 or older who have been exposed to the Internet campaign for President are more likely to engage in voluntary community activities than those who have not been exposed to the Internet campaign for President. These results suggest that the “community involvement” and “post media exposure and attention via Internet” variables are associated.

However, we can not conclude that Internet use for political information has a positive influence on civic engagement in the public sphere, since we have not performed experimental study. It may well be possible that civic engagement and Internet are reciprocally and jointly determined rather than one causing the other.

This study has some limitations because we included only one type of civic engagement (i.e. voluntary work) and one type of Internet use (i.e. use the Internet to obtain information about politics). Another limitation is that data does not include various types of Internet use, such as campaign websites and e-government, and information technology factors such as Internet experience and broadband use.

It is necessary to distinguish different use of the Internet and diversity of civic engagement to examine their relationship correctly. Future research could include additional variables that can further shed light on the true nature of the link between civic engagement and use of the Internet. It would be interesting to include a variety of forms of civic engagement, such as voting, working in election campaigns for political parties, contributing to political causes and candidates, contacting public officials, attending public meetings, political rallies, protests or speeches, signing petitions, serving local organizations and many others.
